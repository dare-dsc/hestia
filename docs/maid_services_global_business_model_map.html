The 5 Model Shapes Running Globally
Model 1 · Franchise
The Brand Promise
Molly Maid, Merry Maids, The Maids (US/Canada/UK)
~$855K avg revenue/unit 90% repeat customers 3–6.5% royalty
Pure recurring subscription economics. Franchise owner runs local ops; brand provides the trust proxy. Initial investment $140K–$200K. Revenue is cleaning sessions, weekly/biweekly. 90% of Molly Maid's 1.7M annual cleanings come from repeat customers. The model is essentially a recurring revenue SaaS disguised as a cleaning service.
Trust mechanismBrand uniformity → standardised training → replacement guarantee → insurance. You trust the brand, not the individual. The cleaner is anonymous to the model but the brand is not.
Flaw: Capital intensive to franchise. Doesn't work at the price point of developing markets. Doesn't exist in Kenya.
Model 2 · Platform / Marketplace
The Commission Rake
Handy (US), Helpling (Europe), TaskRabbit (IKEA), Homejoy (dead)
15–25% commission $75M rev (TaskRabbit) $96M raised (Helpling)
Aggregator model — no assets, just matchmaking + payments. Takes a cut on each transaction. Helpling's insight: "home cleaning is the hardest vertical but once you win trust, cross-selling everything else is easy." 85% of Helpling's business is recurring. TaskRabbit doubled after IKEA integration; furniture assembly became 10% of bookings.
Trust mechanismReviews + ratings + background checks + insurance + payments through platform. Platform holds the money flow as proof of trust.
Fatal flaw: Disintermediation. Cleaners build relationships with households then go direct. Homejoy killed by this + misclassification lawsuits ($6M Handy settlement for 25K workers). See "Fatal Leak" tab.
Model 3 · Full-Stack Platform
The Infrastructure Owner
Urban Company (India → UAE, Singapore, Saudi Arabia)
INR 1,144 crore FY25 (~$137M) 50K+ workers 59 cities First profitable FY25
Not a marketplace. Owns the worker experience end-to-end: multi-day training, tools, uniform, insurance, income predictability, police verification + facial recognition on arrival, community-verified reviews. Workers are micro-entrepreneurs, not gig workers. Trust is solved at every layer of the stack, not just at matching.
Trust mechanismGovernment ID → police verification → skill assessment → multi-day training → on-arrival selfie facial recognition matched to profile → post-job review. Trust is a process, not a score.
Tension: 2024 worker protests in Bengaluru over qualifying fees + account blocking. Workers treated as micro-entrepreneurs in positioning but as exploited gig workers in practice at margin.
Model 4 · Premium Agency
The High-Touch Broker
Sashley Nannies, Dovecare Africa, Nurturing Nest, BestCare (Kenya)
KSh 2,000–10,000 placement fee KSh 1,500/mo retainer Interview commitment fee
Human-led matching. Personal interview, reference checks, training programs, replacement guarantee within a defined period. Revenue = placement fee + monthly agency fee (while the hire is active). Revenue is fragile: hire leaves = retainer gone. Business is as stable as its retention rate.
Trust mechanismHuman vetting (staff who meet every worker personally), reference checks, interview process. Trust is mediated by the agency's reputation and relationship with the employer.
Flaw: Doesn't scale. Every placement requires human time. Monthly retainer only lasts while placement holds. No network effects. High repeat cost per hire.
Model 5 · Digital Profile Marketplace
The Transparency Layer
Msichana.co.ke (Kenya) · browse + unlock model
KSh 200 profile unlock Employer reviews Browse-first UX
You browse hundreds of verified profiles, compare by region/salary/type/reviews, then pay a small fee to unlock contact details. The model flips the traditional agency's "we assign you someone" to "you choose who you want." Positions against traditional bureaus with transparency, speed (hours not days), and low-risk entry price.
Trust mechanismID verification + reference checks + employer reviews. Thinner than Urban Company but directionally correct. The review layer is the trust engine — same mechanic as Airbnb, Uber, TaskRabbit.
Gap: Thin on identity verification depth. No on-arrival accountability. No training standard. Reviews only accumulate after hires — trust at the first hire (highest-risk moment) is still thin.
Model 6 · Informal / Social Media
The Unstructured Default
Facebook groups, WhatsApp networks, Jiji, Pigiame, word-of-mouth (Kenya)
Zero platform fees Zero verification Dominant by volume
The majority of Kenya's domestic worker hiring still happens here. Free, immediate, wide supply. No accountability, no vetting, fake profiles common. The comment section of Mother Goose Kenya's pricing page is a live job board — workers posting phone numbers, employers stating requirements. The informal market is the real incumbent.
Trust mechanismCommunity social proof — someone your cousin knows. Tribal/network trust. No digital infrastructure. Breaks completely for diaspora employers, expats, or new residents with no network.
Not a flaw — it's the baseline: Any formal product must be better than the free informal option. The informal market sets the floor, not the ceiling.

Has worked before, been reviewed honestly by prior employers, built a portable reputation. This is where platforms build moat. Reviews are network-effect infrastructure — each review makes the next hire cheaper.

Identity Commitment — The Long Relationship
The trusted worker who has been with a family for years. "I stayed with my nanny for 13 years." This is what the market actually wants but rarely designs for. The franchise model manufactures this feeling through brand. Community referral produces it organically — slowly. Digital platforms rarely reach it.
Word-of-mouth referral: the only path to level 5 in Kenya today. Molly Maid: brand substitutes for personal relationship. Urban Company: attempting through micro-entrepreneur model. The retention problem nobody is solving in Kenya.
The structural gap in Kenya: Every local player is solving trust at levels 1–3. The platforms (Msichana) are attempting level 4. Nobody is engineering for level 5 — the long relationship — which is the only level that produces retention, referral, and defensible economics.
Forces Shaping This Territory
Trust, Identity & Reputation Force
"Trust becomes infrastructure when proximity disappears." In digital environments, reputational systems lower economic friction across transactions. Where trust accumulates, acquisition gets cheaper. Where trust collapses, friction explodes.
In KenyaCommunity proximity (referral networks) IS the trust infrastructure. Digital trust is attempting to replace physical proximity but hasn't matched the conviction. The viral theft story (Tuko.co.ke) is a trust-collapse event that resets the entire market's firmware simultaneously.
Trust-Gradient Diffusion Force
"Innovation spreads socially before it spreads economically." New categories move through bridges, opinion leaders, and layered trust sequences. Adoption is carried socially before it normalises economically.
In KenyaMsichana's review model is trying to diffuse trust digitally. But it's asking for identity-level commitment (let this person into my home) with awareness-level proof (a few online reviews). The trust transport mechanism hasn't reached the nodes that matter.
Positioning Gravity Force
"No product is judged in a vacuum." Every product is judged inside the buyer's existing comparison set — whether you define it or not. Many failures are failures of frame, not raw product weakness.
In KenyaEvery agency is being compared to "my cousin's recommendation." Until a new operator creates a new comparison set — "the Urban Company of Kenya" — all digital platforms are judged against free word-of-mouth. The positioning battle is being lost before the product is evaluated.
Disintermediation Force
In high-touch, recurring, trust-heavy services, the relationship value accrues to the worker-customer pair, not the platform. Every recurring relationship is a departure waiting to happen.
In KenyaThis force is already operating — clients who find good workers through bureaus stop paying the monthly retainer the moment the relationship is established. The Mother Goose KSh 1,500/month fee is the friction. Good workers = agencies losing revenue. The business model punishes its own success.
Network Effects & Liquidity Force
Platforms only become valuable when both sides have enough density. A marketplace with thin supply or thin demand is just an expensive directory.
In KenyaMsichana lists hundreds of profiles but most show "No employer reviews yet" — the review layer is thin. Without reviews, the platform is a directory, not a trust infrastructure. The network effect is in its infancy. This is the most critical gap: whoever accumulates the first 10,000 employer reviews owns the territory.
Demand Aggregation & Recurring Revenue Force
The value of a service business is a function of its repeat rate, not its acquisition rate. Single transactions fund nothing; recurring relationships compound.
In KenyaThe recurring revenue model barely exists formally. Molly Maid runs on 90% repeat customers and $855K/unit revenue. Kenyan agencies charge a one-time placement fee that evaporates when the hire leaves. Nobody has built the subscription layer that converts a placement into a long-term revenue stream.
How They Position — The Claims and the Reality
Molly Maid / Merry Maids
Franchise · US/Canada
"Professional. Consistent. Insured."
Positioning: the brand as trust. You don't hire a person, you hire a system. Recurring service, team cleaning (never alone), uniformed, insured. The individual is invisible; the brand is everything.
TaskRabbit / IKEA
Platform · Global
"Book a Tasker. Get things done."
Positioning: convenience + control. You pick the person, the time, the price. The IKEA integration shifted the core from "trust a stranger" to "get your furniture assembled after buying it" — a non-threatening entry task that builds the relationship for bigger, more intimate work later.
Urban Company
Full-Stack · India
"Professionals at your doorstep."
Positioning: professionalisation. Not a maid — a professional. The word is deliberate. It reframes the worker's identity, the employer's expectation, and the platform's value proposition. You're not trusting a stranger; you're booking a credentialed professional. Same move Uber made with "ride."
Msichana.co.ke
Digital Platform · Kenya
"You choose. Not the bureau."
Positioning: anti-agency. The claim is transparency and control vs. the traditional bureau's black box. Smart framing — directly addresses the key employer frustration with agencies. But the trust depth (reviews, verification) is thin. Control without trust is a directory, not a marketplace.
Gap: Positioning is correct but trust infrastructure is underbuilt.
Nurturing Nest / BestCare
Premium Agency · Kenya
"Vetted. Trained. Professional."
Positioning: premium trust broker. Differentiates on process quality — thorough vetting, training, replacement guarantees. Targets upper-middle class Nairobi. Sells peace of mind as the product, not the worker.
Gap: Doesn't scale. Every hire requires human time. No network effect.
Mother Goose Kenya
CBO / Training Agency · Kenya
"Professional nannies. Trained. Ethical."
Positioning: training as the trust signal. Requires ECDE diploma, nanny school certification. Adds worker welfare dimension (NHIF, NSSF compliance). Positions at intersection of worker development and employer safety. Revenue model: placement + monthly retainer.
Gap: Revenue model erodes when good placements become direct relationships.
The positioning white space in Kenya: Nobody has claimed "Professionals" as a category. Msichana is closest but stops at "transparency." The operator who says "we don't place maids — we credential domestic professionals" and builds the infrastructure to back it will own an entirely new comparison set.
The Fatal Leak — Why Platforms Fail in This Category

Disintermediation: The Force That Kills Platforms in High-Touch Services

When you put a cleaner and a household together repeatedly, the relationship value accumulates between the two parties — not on the platform. Every trust platform in this space is fighting the gravity of the direct relationship. The platform's 15–25% cut becomes friction with no corresponding value once the relationship exists. The rational move for both parties is to go direct.

Evidence Across the Territory
Homejoy (US) · Dead 2015
Raised $40M. Cleaners used the platform to find clients, then set up direct cleaning relationships, bypassing Homejoy entirely. Their best workers graduated into small independent cleaning businesses. The platform's 25% cut made direct work economically irrational to refuse. Killed also by worker misclassification lawsuits that would have destroyed the unit economics. Shut down July 31, 2015.
Handy (US) · $6M Settlement
Paid $6M to settle misclassification lawsuit (25,000 workers, avg. payout <$200). Workers classified as independent contractors had no healthcare, paid leave, or unemployment insurance. As part of settlement, workers now set their own rates and can negotiate directly with clients. The settlement structurally opened the door to the direct relationship Handy was trying to prevent.
Mother Goose Kenya · Revenue Erosion
The KSh 1,500/month agency fee exists only while the placement holds. A successful placement creates a direct relationship — which is exactly what the employer and worker want. The longer the relationship lasts, the less value the agency provides. The business model charges for access to a relationship that, once formed, needs no intermediary. Every long-lasting placement is a revenue leak.
The Market Default (Kenya) · Informal = Direct from Day One
The informal market — WhatsApp, Facebook, word-of-mouth — is structurally direct. No intermediary, no fee, no accountability. This is what every formal player is competing against. The comment section of Mother Goose's pricing page is itself a live disintermediation event: workers posting phone numbers directly to employers on the agency's own page.
Urban Company (India) · The Only Surviving Answer
Urban Company's response to disintermediation is the only one that works at scale: own the worker relationship as deeply as the customer relationship. Workers are micro-entrepreneurs with training, tools, income predictability, and career development — not interchangeable gig workers. The platform has value to the worker even after the customer relationship is established, because the platform provides income certainty, insurance, and a brand that commands higher pay than the worker could charge independently.
The formula that survives disintermediation: The platform must be worth more to the worker than the direct relationship. If the platform only provides customer access, the worker will leave. If the platform provides training, brand reputation, income smoothing, insurance, and career development — the worker has no reason to go direct. Urban Company built this. Nobody in Kenya has.
