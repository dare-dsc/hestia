# Design Thinking Session: Hestia Logo

**Date:** 2026-05-26
**Facilitator:** Duffy
**Design Challenge:** Logo design for Hestia domestic services platform

---

## 🎯 Design Challenge

Design a simple, powerful logo for Hestia — an on-demand domestic services platform launching in Kenya — paired with the catchphrase **"a mother's touch."**

The logo must speak equally to three audiences simultaneously:
- **Employers** — busy Nairobi professionals who want their home cared for reliably
- **Workers** — domestic service professionals who want to be seen as trusted and dignified
- **General public** — brand awareness, first impression, cultural resonance

The logo should not explain the service. It should make people **feel** something before they read a word.

---

## 👥 EMPATHIZE: Understanding Users

### User Insights

**Employers (Primary):**
- Chronic frustration finding reliable domestic help
- Deep anxiety about trust — letting a stranger into their home
- They don't want a service. They want peace of mind.
- Core emotional need: *"I just want the same reliable person every week without the drama."*

**Workers:**
- Currently invisible to the formal market
- No portable reputation, no protection, no dignity
- Want to be seen as professionals, not just labour
- Core emotional need: *"I want to be trusted and valued for my experience."*

**General Public:**
- Culturally, domestic work is deeply familiar across Kenya
- "A mother's touch" is universally understood — everyone has experienced it
- The phrase needs no explanation. It triggers memory and feeling instantly.

### Key Observations

- The brand's core differentiator is **trust** — not cleaning, not convenience
- The logo must carry trust before the name does
- All three audiences share one universal experience: a mother's care
- This is the single bridge between all three groups

## 🎨 DEFINE: Frame the Problem

### Point of View Statement

Every person — employer, worker, and stranger on the street — has felt a mother's care or given it. The logo must trigger that memory instantly, without explanation, because **trust cannot be argued into existence — it can only be felt.**

### How Might We Questions

- How might we make someone feel safe before they've spoken to anyone?
- How might we represent care without showing a cleaning tool or a house?
- How might we create a mark that means the same thing to a Kilimani professional and a domestic worker from Machakos?
- How might we own a visual territory that no other home services brand in East Africa occupies?

### Key Insights

1. The most powerful logos don't show the product — they capture the feeling
2. "A mother's touch" is not about cleaning — it's about **presence and care**
3. The visual identity of Hestia should be **the act of caring contact itself** — not its tools or setting

### Top Concepts

## THE LEAD CONCEPT: The Mark of Contact

**The insight:** "A mother's touch" is literally — one touch. One deliberate, gentle moment of contact between a person and something they care for. Not a full hand. Not an embrace. *Just the touch itself.*

**The visual direction:** A single, minimal abstract mark that captures the moment of intentional contact. Not the home. Not the hand. The act of caring presence itself.

**Possible forms:**
- A single curved line making quiet contact with a surface — still, intentional
- A minimalist mark suggesting a fingertip touching — unique, human, irreplaceable  
- An abstract impression — like the gentle weight of care made visible

**Why this works for all three audiences:**
- Employer: *"Someone is here. They see my home. They care."*
- Worker: *"I bring this. This is my professional mark."*
- Public: *"I don't know what this is yet — but I feel something."*

**Why this is ownable:** No home services brand in Kenya or East Africa uses anything like this. It is category-defying by design.
